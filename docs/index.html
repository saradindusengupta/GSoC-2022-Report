It is already time for the final submission for this year’s GSoC where I was working with The R project of Statistical Computing for the last 3 months and this is the time I ponder over my accomplishments over the period.

Motivation

Since the idea-lists were announced for GSoC, 2022 I was particularly looking for projects which complement my area of expertise and skill-sets. The project idea to understand calendar seasonality specific to Indian calendar systems with ‘VedicDateTime (Part A)’ was very interesting to me, foremost of the reasons being by past experience with work on time-series forecasting and how seasonality changes and alters forecasting over a long period of time.

My experience in working on near real-time data-stream for time-series based forecasting exposed a couple of doubts regarding handling time itself while doing any forecasting or even segmentation based on-trend. Using a separate calendar system to overcome the issues of calendar seasonality would give some understanding of the drawback of the current method of mentioning the time component.

Since I never worked with Indian calendar system earlier, especially on a analysis domain, it was quite challenging to see through the project and decide on convergence of the topic.

During the evaluation phase, I only applied to this project and submitted evaluation. The initial background research conducted during that period helped me gain a fair bit of knowledge regarding Indian calendar system and its various concepts, which itself is a separate topic.

Challenges

One of the primary challenges was understanding Indian calendar system. Unlike a Gregorian calendar system which is a Solar calendar system meaning components of time are decided and maintained by measuring the movement of sun, Indian calendar system is a luni-solar calendar system meaning the day, month and year component of time is based on movement by the sun and the moon.

Journey

Background knowledge

As with the nature of the topic, I had to delve right into understanding Vedic calendar system1 and its various sub-parts as well. How sun’s and moon’s position changes month, year and how moon’s phase determines lunar months were the most important part.

Package development

Initial development

With the basic understanding of the calendar system and with the help of other R libraries (Stubner et al. 2019), built a working version of R functions which can convert a Gregorian calendar date to an equivalent Vedic calendar component.

Re-factoring

After the initial development of basic functions, a significant time went into re-factoring and debugging the existing codebase.

Preparation for CRAN

Unit-testing

Since from the start, the intention was to publish the package to CRAN as there remains to package in R to handle Vedic calendar system. In that regard, based on the GSoC proposal, almost 70% test coverage were achieved.

Documentation

Function Description

All of the library functions were documented with roxygen2 .

Vignettes

Vignettes and supporting documentations were added to make the library much more useful and understandable.
